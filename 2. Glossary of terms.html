Science & Technology, Online Culture, Social Media, and the Age of AI

Key Concepts

Utopian Vision

A positive, hopeful view of the future where technology and society improve people's lives. In a utopian vision, new inventions and social changes solve problems, create opportunities, and make the world a better, fairer, and happier place for everyone.

Dystopian Vision

A negative, worrying view of the future where technology and society create serious problems. In a dystopian vision, new developments lead to increased control, loss of freedom, inequality, and harm to individuals or society. People fear the negative consequences more than they celebrate the benefits.

AI slop
Low-quality content that is created quickly and carelessly by artificial intelligence. This content often feels generic, unoriginal, or poorly made, flooding the internet with mediocre material that wastes people's time.
algorithm
A set of rules or instructions that a computer follows to solve a problem or make decisions. On social media, algorithms decide what content you see in your feed based on your past behavior and preferences.
algorithmic bias
When computer systems make unfair decisions because they learned from biased data or were programmed with human prejudices. This can lead to discrimination against certain groups of people in areas like hiring, lending, or policing.
augmented reality
Technology that adds digital information or images to what you see in the real world, usually through your phone or special glasses. For example, Pok√©mon GO shows virtual creatures in real locations.
automation
When machines or computers do work that humans used to do. This can make processes faster and cheaper, but it may also eliminate jobs and change the nature of work.
autonomous vehicles
Cars, trucks, or other vehicles that can drive themselves without a human driver. They use sensors, cameras, and AI to navigate roads and make decisions.
biotechnology
The use of living organisms or biological processes to develop new products or solve problems. This includes things like genetic engineering, creating new medicines, or developing crops that resist disease.
brain rot
A slang term describing the mental decline or loss of focus caused by consuming too much low-quality online content. People feel their thinking becomes less sharp when they spend too much time on mindless scrolling or watching trivial videos.
cancel culture
The practice of publicly calling out and boycotting people (often celebrities or public figures) for behavior or statements considered offensive or problematic. This can lead to them losing their job, reputation, or platform.
citizen journalism
When ordinary people report news and share information through social media and blogs, rather than professional journalists working for traditional media companies. Anyone with a phone can document and share events as they happen.
content moderation
The process of reviewing and removing inappropriate, harmful, or rule-breaking content from websites and social media platforms. This can be done by human moderators or AI systems.
cyberbullying
Using the internet, social media, or digital devices to harass, threaten, or embarrass someone. This can include sending mean messages, spreading rumors, or posting embarrassing photos without permission.
data privacy
The right to control what information about you is collected, how it's used, and who can see it. This includes protecting personal details like your location, browsing history, and private messages.
deep learning
A type of artificial intelligence that learns patterns from huge amounts of data, similar to how human brains learn. It's used for things like recognizing faces in photos, understanding speech, or translating languages.
digital divide
The gap between people who have access to modern technology (computers, internet, smartphones) and those who don't. This creates inequality because people without access miss out on opportunities for education, work, and information.
digital footprint
The trail of data you leave behind when using the internet. This includes your social media posts, comments, photos, search history, and any information that can be traced back to you online.
digital literacy
The ability to use technology effectively and understand how to find, evaluate, and create information online. It also includes knowing how to stay safe and identify fake news or scams.
doomscrolling
Spending excessive amounts of time reading bad news online, even though it makes you feel anxious or depressed. People continue scrolling through negative content even when they know it's harming their mental health.
echo chamber
A situation where you only hear opinions and information that match what you already believe. This happens when algorithms or your choice of friends/media sources reinforce your existing views without exposing you to different perspectives.
facial recognition
Technology that can identify or verify a person by analyzing their face. This is used for unlocking phones, security systems, or tracking people in public spaces.
filter bubble
When websites and social media only show you content that matches your interests and previous behavior, creating a "bubble" where you see a limited view of the world. This can prevent you from encountering diverse ideas or perspectives.
influencer culture
The phenomenon where social media personalities (influencers) have significant impact on their followers' opinions, purchasing decisions, and behavior. These people make money by promoting products or lifestyles to their audience.
machine learning
A type of AI where computers learn from experience and improve their performance over time without being explicitly programmed. For example, spam filters learn to recognize unwanted emails by analyzing thousands of examples.
meme culture
The creation and sharing of humorous images, videos, or text that spread rapidly online. Memes often comment on current events, popular culture, or shared experiences, creating a form of digital communication and community.
misinformation
False or inaccurate information that spreads online, whether shared accidentally or intentionally. This can include fake news, manipulated photos, or out-of-context quotes that mislead people.
neural networks
Computer systems designed to work like the human brain, with interconnected "neurons" that process information. These are used in AI to recognize patterns, make predictions, and learn from data.
online activism
Using social media and the internet to raise awareness about social or political issues, organize protests, or push for change. This can include hashtag campaigns, online petitions, or coordinating real-world actions.
personalized medicine
Medical treatment that is customized for each individual patient based on their genes, lifestyle, and environment. This allows doctors to choose treatments that are most likely to work for that specific person.
predictive policing
Using data analysis and AI to predict where crimes are likely to happen or who might commit crimes. Police departments use this to decide where to patrol, but critics worry it reinforces existing biases.
quantum computing
An extremely powerful type of computing that uses principles of quantum physics to process information much faster than regular computers. This could revolutionize fields like medicine, cryptography, and scientific research.
renewable energy
Energy that comes from natural sources that don't run out, like sunlight, wind, or water. Unlike fossil fuels, renewable energy doesn't pollute the environment and can be used indefinitely.
social credit system
A government system (used in some countries like China) that monitors and rates citizens' behavior, rewarding "good" behavior and punishing "bad" behavior. This can affect people's ability to travel, get loans, or access services.
surveillance capitalism
A business model where companies collect huge amounts of data about people's behavior and use it to predict and influence what they will do next, primarily to sell targeted advertising or products.
technological unemployment
Job loss caused when machines, robots, or AI systems replace human workers. As technology advances, certain jobs become automated, leaving workers without employment.
telemedicine
Healthcare services delivered remotely through video calls, phone consultations, or online platforms. Patients can talk to doctors and receive medical advice without visiting a clinic or hospital in person.
universal basic income
A proposed system where the government regularly gives all citizens a fixed amount of money, regardless of whether they work or not. This aims to provide financial security, especially as automation eliminates jobs.
viral content
Photos, videos, or posts that spread extremely quickly across the internet, being shared by millions of people in a short time. Content "goes viral" when it captures widespread attention or emotion.
virtual reality
Technology that creates a completely digital environment that you can explore and interact with using special headsets and controllers. It makes you feel like you're actually inside a different world.
